5 Weirdest Christmas Traditions

Number 5: A Christmas Remembrance, Finland.

On Christmas Eve, families often visit the graves of their ancestors to light candles in the memory of the deceased. Even if families do not have their ancestors graves nearby them, they will still leave out candles in nearby graveyards that honour their ancestors who are buried elsewhere.

Number 4: Let the Goat Live Until Christmas, Sweden.

In 1996, a 13-metre tall goat figure made out of straw was built in the town of Gävle. At the stroke of midnight on Christmas Eve, the goat went up in flames. However, the town never stopped building the goat, year after year. By 2011, the goat had been burnt down 25 times. The burning of the Gävle goat happened so often that bookmakers began taking bets on the survival of the goat 1988.

Number 3: Toss your shoe and get hitched. Czech Republic and Slovakia.

This tradition is very similar to the wedding tradition where brides would throw their bouquet and whoever caught the bouquet would be the next person to get married. However, in Czech and Republic and Slovakia, this involves a shoe, and it needs to be on Christmas day. The person would stand with their back towards the door and then throw their shoe over their shoulder. If the shoe lands with the toe pointing towards the door, then that person is then deemed to be married soon.

Number 2: Go Bananas with Christmas, India.

In India, only 2.3 percent of the population are Christians, but if you think about that, it's an enormous amount of people considering their population. In fact, that's about 25 million people. Instead of celebrating Christmas with the normal Christmas trees made out of fir or pine to decorate, they use banana trees and even mango trees.

Number 1: Kentucky for Christmas, Japan.

Instead of the usual turkey Christmas dinner, the Japanese feast on the Colonel's Kentucky Fried Chicken, all thanks to a campaign in 1974. KFC has been associated with Christmas in the minds of the Japanese for generations, a tradition passed from parent to child. More than 240,000 barrels of chicken will be sold during Christmas, five to ten times normal monthly sales.
